Overview

My principal findings were straight-forward:

  • First, in 2014 there were approximately 4,125,164 employees in the entire U.S. information technology sector ==> 300,431 were Black, and 277,280 were Hispanic.

  • Second, the percentage of tech employees who were Black was higher in states that had larger Black populations and smaller in states that had smaller Black populations. The same relationship between population size and the percentage of tech employment held true for Hispanics.

  • Third, if all other things were equal, one would expect that the percentage of employment in a state’s tech sector that was Black would be about the same as the percentage of the state’s total population that was Black. One would would expect similar parity for the percentages of Hispanics. Unfortunately, all of things were not equal, so I found that the percentage of the tech sector of most states that was Black or Hispanic was substantially smaller than the percentage of the state’s total population that was Black or Hispanic. By contrast, I found that the percentage of state tech sectors that were Asian ranged as high as 4.4 times the percentage of the state’s total population that was Asian.

  • Fourth, the strengths of these relationships varied widely: some states offered substantially larger employment opportunities for Blacks and Hispanics in their information technology sectors than others.

Question 4 – Where did White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic techs live in 2014?

Answer 4 … The following paragraphs provide an extensive response to a more specific formulation of this question ==> In which states did White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic techs reside in 2014? My underlying assumption was that most people worked in the state in which they lived. So the number of tech residents in a state should be about the same as the number of tech jobs in a state. (Readers are reminded of two notable exceptions: New York City and Washington, DC wherein substantial numbers of out-of-state workers commute from nearby New Jersey and Connecticut, and from Maryand and Viginia, respectively.)

I’ve presented the U.S. Census data that answers this question in two formats: as a set of choropleth maps and as a corresponding set of tables. I show the maps first because it’s easier to see the residential patterns of the racial/ethnic groups by looking at multicolored maps than by scanning columns of data in long tables. Indeed, a quick scan of the maps makes it easier to see the same patterns in the data tables.

There are four maps (below), one for each racial/ethnic group. Map 4A shows the residential distribution of White techs; Map 4B shows the distribution of Black techs; Map 4C shows Asian techs; and Map 4D shows Hispanics. Each map colors the states with the highest number of tech residents in the darkest colors, and colors the states with the least tech residents in the lightest colors.

Maps 4A (Whites), 4B (Blacks), 4C (Asians), and 4D (Hispanics) … Lower 48 and DC

  • Map 4A shows that White techs are spread across a large number of states, with the highest number living in California, second highest in Texas.

  • Map 4B shows that most Black techs are located in the Eastern and Southern states, with the largest number living in Texas, second in Georgia, and third in Virginia

  • Map 4C shows that Asian techs are highly concentrated in California where their concentration is higher any other group in any state. Otherwise Asians are spread out over other states with much smaller Asian residencies.

  • Map 4D shows that Hispanics are concentrated in California, Texas, and Florida

Four data tables impose numeric precision on the qualitative insights provided by the corresponding maps. The tables are ordered by the number of White/Black/Asian/Hispanic techs in each state. The short versions that only contain the top ten rows are displayed below. To view the full tables that contain data for all 50 states plus the District of Columbia, please click the link below the table name. Each table (full and short version) has the following eight columns

1. State – State of residence for the techs whose data is shown in the other columns of each row

2. AllTech – Data for all of the techs in the U.S. The data in this column is repeated in each of the four tables, but the order of the rows differs from one table to the next. For example Table 4A shows that the total number of techs in California in 2014 was 576,333, and the total number in Texas was 326,437

3. S_% – Percent of all U.S. techs in this racial group who lived in this state. For example, Table 4A shows that 9.5 percent of all White techs lived in California in 2014, and 6.8 percent lived in Texas.

4. WhiTech, BlkTech, AsiTech, or HispTech – White techs, Black techs, Asian techs, or Hispanic techs in tables A, B, C, and D. The data in this column shows the number of techs from the table’s racial/ethnic group that lived in each state. For example, Table 4A shows that 253,426 White techs lived in California, and 180,686 White techs lived in Texas. The rows of each table are ordered by data in this column, i.e., by the number of techs in each state; states with higher numbers at the top, lower numbers at the bottom.

5. T_% – Percent of of the state’s techs who were in the table’s racial/ethnic group. For exampleTable 4A shows that 44 percent of the techs in California were White, and 55.4 percent of the techs in Texas were White.

6. WhiPop, BlkPop, AsiPop, or HspPop – White population, Black population, Asian population, or Hispanic population in the entire United States. For example Table 4A shows that the 2014 White (non-Hiispanic) population of California was 14,920,185, and the White (non-Hispanic) population of Texas was 11,735,074.

7. P_% – Percent of the state’s total population that belonged to the table’s racial/ethnic group. For example, Table 4A shows that in 2014, 38.5 percent of the total population of California was White, and 43.5 percent of the total population of Texas was white

8. Par – Parity ratio of the percent of the state’s tech population that belonged to the table’s racial/ethnic group divided by the percent of the state’s total population that belonged to that group, i.e., T_% / P_%. For example, Table 4A shows that the White parity ratio for California was 1.14, which is divided by 38.5

Comments about Table 4B – Blacks in Tech

  • As I noted earlier, New York City and Washington, DC have a large daytime commuter populations. Therefore the number of Black techs who worked in New York state or Washington, DC in 2014 may have been substantially larger than the number of Black techs listed in Table 4B.

  • Column 4 (BlkTech) of Table 4B (above) shows that in 2014 the largest number of Black techs lived in Texas, second largest in Georgia, third in Virginia, fourth in New York, fifth in Maryland, sixth in Florida, and California placed seventh. Why? Because in 2014 the vast majority Black Americans still lived in Eastern and Southern states; so that’s where most of the Black techs were found. This put most Black techs at a considerable disadvantage with regards to learning about and/or being recruited for tech jobs in Silicon Valley.

  • California’s prominence as the corporate headquarters of many of the nation’s richest tech companies makes it necessary to closely examine the status of its minorities. Unfortunately the numbers were disappointing. As per the fifth column (T_%), the 2.9% Black share of tech in California was the lowest in the table by a wide margin. And its parity numbers in the last column (Par) were lower than all but one of the other nine states.

  • Perhaps California’s 2,220,275 Black population was too small compared to its largest-in-the-nation 38,952,193 total population to produce enough qualified Black techs to earn a larger slice of the pie? Unfortunately, this hypothesis is undermined by even worse numbers for Hispanics in Table D. By 2014 Hispanics had become the largest racial/ethnic group in the state, larger than Whites. But as will be seen in Table 4D (below), the Hispanic parity values were much lower than the Black parity values.

Comments about Table 4D – Hispanics in Tech

  • As per the third column (S_%) of Table 4D, Hispanic techs were concentrated in three states: California, Texas, and Florida. The table shows that 21 percent lived in California, 15.5 percent lived in Texas, and 12.1 percent lived in Florida. Taken together, these three states accounted for 48.6 percent of the Hispanic techs in the entire country, i.e., about half. The other half live in the other 47 states and the District of Columbia.

  • It is interesting to note that all of the parity values of the top 10 states for Black techs shown in the last column of Table 4B are substantially higher than the parity values for all of the top 10 states for Hispanic techs shown in Table 4D … with one exception: Florida, parity = 0.69. Florida’s parity value for Hispanic techs was more than twice the 0.26 parity value for Hispanics in California. Indeed, Table 4D shows that California had the lowest parity value of all of the top ten states for Hispanics. These considerations suggest the possbility that Hispanic techs may have encountered larger employment barriers than Black techs, especially in California.

  • Despite California’s relatively low parity, the fifth column (T_%) of Table 4D shows that 10.1 percent of the techs who lived in California were Hispanic. This figure is remarkable because it is two to three times as high as the 2 percent to 4 percent employment rates for Hispanic techs that have been reported by Silicon Valley’s biggest firms in the last three years. It suggests that willingness to hire Hispanic techs was low throughout the state, but substantantially lower in the Valley.

  • Perhaps the Valley required higher academic qualifications than other employers. But as I pointed out in a recent note on this blog (“The California Pipelines of Silicon Valley”), IPEDS data showed that 20 percent of the combined total of White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic students enrolled in the best colleges and universities in California were Hispanic. So there is reason to be skeptical about claims that very few Hispanic techs met the Valley’s higher recruiting standards.

Question 5 – Was the size of a racial/ethnic group related to the group’s share of the tech jobs in a stte?

Answer 5: As shown in four plots that follow, there was a positive relationship, but the relationship was strongest for Asians, weaker for Whites, still weaker for Blacks, and weakest for Hispanics

Plots 5 – Plots of Tech Employment vs Race/Ethnic Group Population

Comments about Plots 5A, 5B, 5C, and 5D

  • The total population of each state was at least 1,000 times the population of the techs in any racial/ethnic group. Therefore I ran simple regressions of techs in a group against the total population of that group divided by 1000 in all 50 states plus Washington, DC. The slopes of the regression lines provided estimates of the number of additional techs that would be found in a state if the size of the group increased by 1000. Assuming that the vast majority of techs worked in the same states in which they lived, the slopes provide estimates of the number of additional tech residents the group would include if its population increased by 1,000.

  • The regression lines and scatterplots of the data points appear in the four panels. The values of the slopes, called “beta1000”, are displayed in the upper left corner of each plot.

  • As per the values of the beta1000 slopes, If each ethnic/racial group’s population increased by 1,000, Asian techs would increase by 42.84; Whites would increase by 14.28, Blacks would increase by 7.37; and Hispanics would increase by 4.13. The Asian line has the steepest slope; the Hispanic line has the flattest slope. Note that the point on the far right side of the line in Plot B for Black techs was not a real data point; it was added to extrapolate what was otherwise a very short line in order to make the slope of the line more visible.

  • This kind of simple regression implicitly assumes that the 50 states and DC are all more or less equally receptive or resistant to including more techs from the same groups. This may indeed be true for most states. However, Table 4B shows that the Black parity for Texas = 0.8, whereas the Black parity for California = 0.51. This suggests that Texas is more receptive to Black techs than California. And Table 4D shows that the Hispanic parity for Florida = 0.69, while the Hispanic parity for California = 0.26. This suggests that Florida is more receptive to Hispanic techs than California.

Comments about Table 5

  • The parity and beta1000 statistics shown in the table provide different ways to measure the same concept ==> The relative advantages that the nation as a whole provided to the four racial/ethnic groups in 2014.

  • The parity ratios shown in the table are the simple ratios of each group’s percentage share of the tech sector in the entire country to the group’s percentage share of the total population in the entire country, i.e., column 2 (T_%) / column 4 (S_%). The more complex beta1000 statistics were the results of regressions that estimated a linear relationship between the size of a group’s tech population (T_% in Tables A, B, C, and D) in every state to the group’s total population in every state (S_% in Tables A, B, C, and D).

  • As can be seen from the last two columns in the table, the two measures provided the same results: Asians were the most advantaged {parity = 3.6, beta1000 = 42.84 }, Whites second {parity = 1.04, beta1000 = 14.28}, Blacks third {parity = 0.59, beta100 = 7.37}, and Hispanics were the least advantaged {parity = 0.39, beta100 = 4.13}.

Conclusions

Which states were the best states for Black and Hispanic tech? Given the limited scope of the data that I examined in this study, my selection criteria were rudimentary; so my conclusions should be regarded as tentative first approximations.

  1. Select states that had the largest number of Black/Hispanic tech
    I implemented this criterion by selecting the states with the ten highest numbers of techs in each group. This meant that I began with the states that appeared in Table 4B (Black) and Table 4D (Hispanic).

  2. Select states that had the high Black/Hispanic parity values
    I implemented this criterion by identifying the states in Tables 4B and 4D that had parity values in the top 50% of all 50 states plus DC. Table 6 (below) summarizes the quartile breakdowns of the parity values for each racial/ethnic group. As per Table 6, the median Black parity value was 0.56; the median Hispanic parity value was 0.39.

Motivation

My motivation for conducting this study influenced my methods. The recent barrage of annual reports from some of the nation’s leading information technology companies in Silicon Valley say the primary reason why those companies employed so few Black and Hispanic information technology professionals was because there were so few available. And the reason they weren’t available was because there were so few Black and Hispanic tech students in the nation’s academic pipelines.

When reading their reports I found myself shaking my head while wondering, “What numbers did they have to back up these assertions? And where did they get them from?” Whereas I would have said that the pipelines were half-full, these reports implied that the pipelines were perilously close to empty. But if the pipelines were close to empty, there shouldn’t be very many Black or Hispanic employees in the U.S. tech sector, right? Which led me to wonder how many Black and Hispanic techs were out there and where were they located.

One of my most important take-aways from completing the Johns Hopkins “Data Science” online certificate program a few months ago was the notion that reports should be “reproducible”. Authors should not only describe their methods for collecting and analyzing data; they should provide detailed step-by-step descriptions of their methods (including copies of their code) so that interested readers could readily reproduce the results on their own computers. Of course, the best researchers have always tried to achieve this objective and have often succeeded. What was new for me was the specific tools that the statisticians who taught the Hopkins courses recommended ==> the R programming language and GitHub.

  • Data should be downloaded from original sources, then analyzed, tabulated, mapped, and graphed via programs writen in R. Findings should be summarized in text laid out in a subset of Markdown that an R application called “knitr” converts to HTML pages suitable for publication on Websites.

  • Reproducible reports should be as concise as traditional reports, but the writer’s notes, programs, analytical tools, coding guides, data files, tables, maps, graphs, etc, etc, etc should be shared with the world via GitHub repositories. Of course Python, MATLAB, and other comparable languages could be used insted of R, and the supplementary files could be stored on other public repositories comparable to GitHub.

This being my first attept to write a reproducible report, I wrote it in R and stored all of my supporting files in a public GitHub repo. If readers disagree with my numbers, they won’t have to wonder how I got them … #OldDogStillLearningNewTricks … :-)

Methods

I used the U.S. Census Bureau’s DataFerret Java application to download a subset of the Public Use Microdata Sample (PUMS) from the Bureau’s American Community Survey for 2014. The subset (39,692 observations) was usually large enough to make good estimates of the employee counts and percentages of tech employment for Blacks, Whites, Asians, and Hispanics in tech occupations in the 50 states and DC. (Note: For a few low population states, the ACS subset included missing values which meant that it wasn’t large to provide reliable estimates of the racial/ethnic breakdown of tech employment in those states.) My step-by-step procedures for selecting and downloading the ACS data and its codebook is described in Appendix #1 _ ACS Downloads on the GitHub repo.

I also downloaded the #PEPSR6H data table from the Bureau’s American Fact Finder Website. This table contains the Bureau’s estimates of the overall racial composition of the populations of the 50 states plus Washington DC in 2014.

Selected Data
I used the DataFerret to download a subset of the PUMS 2014 sample about the sex, state of residence, race, and Hispanic status of employees in the tech sector in all 50 states plus the District of Columbia:

  • Occupation
    When using the DataFerret app to select a sample of tech professionals from the full PUMS 2014 sample, I defined the “information technology sector” by designating the 13 standard occupational categories shown in Table 3 (above). Eleven categories were software, two were hardware

  • Hispanic vs White (not Hispanic), Black (not Hispanic), Asian (not Hispanic)
    I configured the DataFerret to include all races in my sample. However, Hispanic is not regarded as a race, but as an ethnic/national origin, like being Irish, Egyptian, Japanese, or Nigerian. So I included the “Hispanic” variable. This variable designated each respondent’s Hispanic origin, e.g., Mexico, Chile, Puerto Rico, etc … but it also contained a “Not Hispanic” value for respondents who were not from Hispanic countries. Using this code, I distinguished between “White” vs. “White (not Hispanic)”, “Black” vs. “Black (not Hispanic)”, and “Asian” vs. “Asian (not Hispanic)” and “Others” vs “Others (non-Hispanic)”. Accordingly, my report contains data about four racial/ethnic groups: Hispanic, White (not Hispanic), Black (not Hispanic), and Asian (not Hispanic); but for brevity it merely calls them Hispanic, White, Black, and Asian.

  • Estimating population values from the sample
    Each row in the downloaded PUMS table contained the anonymized responses of a real tech employee in the ACS 2014 survey, i.e., sex, race/ethnicity, occupation, and state of residence. Each observation also contained a number called “personal weight” which is the Census Bureau’s estimate of how many people in the real population were like each person in the sample. An estimate of the total number of techs in the real population was found by adding up the personal weights of the techs in the sample. This also applied to subgroups, e.g, male vs. female, state of residencere, etc. The percentage share of a subgroup’s residence was obtained by dividing the estimated residence in the subgroup by the estimated residence in the entire group, i.e., dividing the sum of personal weights in a subgroup by the sum of personal weights in the larger group.

    Here’s the Census Bureau’s description of this process:
    “Production of Estimates” – The ACS PUMS sample is not self - weighted. To produce estimates or tabulations of characteristics from the ACS PUMS simply add the weights of all persons or housing units that possess the characteristic of interest. For instance, if the characteristic of interest is “total number of black teachers”, simply determine the race and occupation of all persons and cumulate the weights of those who match the characteristics of interest. To get estimates of proportions simply divide the weighted estimate of persons or housing units with a given characteristic by the weighted estimate of the base. For example, the proportion of “black teachers” is obtained by dividing the weighted estimate of black teachers by the PUMS estimate of teachers."

I used R Studio’s IDE to write the R scripts and functions that produced the report’s tables, maps, graphs, and stats. My R code can be found in the Data-1A.R, Data-1B.R, Stats-2.R, and Report-3.Rmd files in the Best States repo, as well as copies of the codebooks and .csv files I downloaded from the Census. I composed the text of the report in R-Markdown, embedded the data objects, converted the R-markdown to HTML via knitr, pushed the report to a git-io pages, then made the pages accessible to the DLL’s Tech-Levers blog via an iframe on an otherwise blank page on the blog.

The scripts and embedded functions should be run in numerical order ==> Data-1A.R and Data-1B.R create the data frames required by Stats-2.R; Stats-2.R creates the tables, maps, graphs, and regression stats required by the R-markdown script in the Report-4.Rmd file that generates the report.
