Overview

My principal findings were straight-forward:

  • First, in 2014 there were approximately 4,125,164 employees in the entire U.S. information technology sector ==> 300,431 were Black; 277,280 were Hispanic; and 1,002,113 were female. (See Table 1A, Table 1B, Table 2A, and Table 2B.)

  • Second, the number of tech employees who were Black was higher in states that had larger Black populations and smaller in states that had smaller Black populations. The same relationship between population size and tech employment held true for Hispanics and females. (See Table 4B, Table 4D, and Table 6)

  • Third, if all other things were equal, one would expect that the percentage of a state’s tech sector that was Black would be about the same as the percentage of the state’s total population that was Black. One would expect similar parity for the percentages of Hispanics and for females. But all things were not equal, so I found that the percentage of the tech sector of most states that was Black, Hispanic, or female was substantially smaller than the percentage of the state’s total population that was Black, Hispanic, or female. By contrast, I found that the percentage of state tech sectors that was Asian was always greater than their population percentage and ranged as high as 8.41 times their population percentage. (See Tables 4C and 6)

  • Fourth, female techs only held 24.3 percent of the nation’s 4,125,164 tech positions. After considering the disproportionate number of tech positions allocated to Asian techs, I split the female tech category into Asian females and non-Asian females. I then found that Asian females also occupied disproportionately larger shares of the tech sectors in most states than their share of each state’s population. Indeed, their tech shares ranged as high as 6.29 times their shares of state populations. (See Table 4E and Table 6.) By contrast, non-Asian females occupied much smaller shares of the state tech sectors than their shares of the state populations. (See Tables 2B, 3 and 6.)

  • Fifth, the strengths of these relationships varied widely: some states offered substantially larger employment opportunities for Blacks, Hispanics, and female techs than others. (See Tables 8B, 8D, 8E, and 8F)

  • Sixth, California and New York, the states that employed the largest and third largest number of information technology professionals, were only “best states” for White information technology professionals; neither was a “best state” for Blacks, Hispanics, Asians, Asian females, or non-Asian females in tech.

Motivation

My motivation for conducting this study influenced my methods. The recent barrage of annual reports from some of the nation’s leading information technology companies in Silicon Valley said the primary reason why those companies employed so few Black, Hispanic, and female information technology professionals was because there were so few available. And the reason they weren’t available was because there were so few Black, Hispanic, and female tech students in the nation’s academic pipelines.

A. Best Practices
When reading the Silicon Valley reports I found myself shaking my head while wondering, “What numbers did they have to back up these assertions? And where did they get them from?” Whereas I would have said that the pipelines were half-full, these reports implied that the pipelines were perilously close to empty. But if the pipelines were close to empty, there shouldn’t be many Black, Hispanic, or female employees in the U.S. tech sector, right? This led me to wonder how many Black, Hispanic, and femalec techs were out there and where they were located.

What concerned me the most was the lack of context for assessing Silicon Valley’s diversity numbers. Were they as good as could be expected? Were they worse? … And compared to what? Compared to the rest of California? Compared to other states in other parts of the country? The personal data that I had accumulated from more than 50 years in tech informed me that tech was much more diverse in the Eastern and Southern states. But personal data, while a useful source of productive hypotheses, does not provide credible, systematic proof. As a clever tech pundit recently proclaimed, “The plural of personal anecdote is not data” … :-)

One of the most useful lessons that I learned as a consultant to the National Center for Productivity way back in the late 1970s was that the application of performance metrics, even flawed metrics, to just about any significant human activity invariably identified a broad middle tier that was above a small tier of low performers and below another small tier of high performers. Further inquiry usually disclosed that the high performers were doing things quite differently than the middle and low performers. Happily, performers in the lower tiers could improv by adopting the high performers’ best practices if they made appropriate adaptations of the best practices to their local working conditions.

Given this insight, I set out to apply a simple measure of the performance of all 50 states and the District of Columbia with regards to the opportunities their tech sectors provided for Black, Hispanic, and female professionals. If Silicon Valley proved to be a low performer in these national contexts, its executives would be unlikely sources of best practices that could improve the Valley’s performance.

B. Reproducible Reports
One of my most important take-aways from completing the Johns Hopkins “Data Science” online certificate program a few months ago was the notion that reports should be “reproducible”. Authors should not only describe their methods for collecting and analyzing data; they should provide step-by-step descriptions of their methods (including copies of their code) so that interested readers could readily reproduce the results on their own computers. Of course, the best researchers have always tried to achieve this objective and have often succeeded. What was new for me was the specific tools that the statisticians who taught the Hopkins courses recommended ==> the R programming language and GitHub.

  • Data should be downloaded from original sources, then analyzed, tabulated, mapped, and graphed via programs writen in R. Findings should be summarized in text laid out in a subset of Markdown that an R application called “knitr” would convert to HTML pages suitable for publication on Websites.

  • Reproducible reports should be as concise as traditional reports, but the writer’s notes, programs, analytical tools, coding guides, data files, tables, maps, and graphs should be shared with the world via GitHub repositories. Of course Python, MATLAB, or other comparable languages could be used insted of R, and the supplementary files could be stored on other public repositories comparable to GitHub.

This being my first attept to write a reproducible report, I wrote it in R and stored my supporting files in a public GitHub repo. If readers disagree with my numbers, they won’t have to wonder how I got them … #OldDogStillLearningNewTricks … :-)

My underlying assumption was that most people worked in the states in which they lived. So the number of tech residents in a state should be about the same as the number of techs employed in that state.

(Readers are reminded of two notable exceptions: New York City and Washington, DC wherein substantial numbers of out-of-state workers commute from nearby New Jersey and Connecticut, and from Maryand and Viginia, respectively.)

Six data tables impose numeric precision on the qualitative insights provided by the corresponding maps. The tables are ordered by the number of techs from each group that lived in each state. The short versions that only contain the top ten rows are displayed below. To view the full tables that contain data for all 50 states plus the District of Columbia, please click the link below the table name. Each table (full and short versions) has the following eight columns:

1. State – State of residence for the techs whose data is shown in the other columns of each row

2. AllTech – Data for all of the techs in the U.S. The data in this column is repeated in each of the six tables, but the order of the rows differs from one table to the next. For example Table 4A shows that the total number of techs in California in 2014 was 576,333, and the total number in Texas was 326,437

3. SS_% – The state’s share of each group’s national total. More specifically, the percent of all U.S. techs in a group that lived in each state. For example, Table 4A shows that 9.53 percent of all White techs lived in California in 2014, and 6.79 percent lived in Texas.

4. WhiTech, BlkTech, AsiTech, HispTech, FemAsiTech, or FemNonAsiTech – White techs, Black techs, Asian techs, Hispanic techs, Asian female techs, and Non-Asian female techs in tables A, B, C, D, E, and F, respectively. The data in this column shows the number of techs from the table’s racial/ethnic group that lived in each state. For example, Table 4A shows that 253,426 White techs lived in California, and 180,686 White techs lived in Texas. The rows of each table are ordered by data in this column, i.e., by the number of techs in each state; states with higher numbers are at the top of the table; states with lower numbers are at the bottom.

5. T_% – Percent of a state’s techs who were in the table’s group. For exampleTable 4A shows that 44 percent of the techs in California were White, and 55.4 percent of the techs in Texas were White.

6. WhiPop, BlkPop, AsiPop, HspPop, FemAsiPop, or FemNonAsiPop – White population, Black population, Asian population, Hispanic population, female Asian population, or female non-Asian population in each state. For example, Table 4A shows that the 2014 White population of California was 14,920,185, and the White population of Texas was 11,735,074.

7. P_% – Percent of the state’s total population that belonged to the table’s group. For example, Table 4A shows that in 2014, 38.5 percent of the total population of California was White, and 43.5 percent of the total population of Texas was white
.
8. Par – Parity = The ratio of the percent of the state’s tech population that belonged to the table’s group divided by the percent of the state’s total population that belonged to that group, i.e., T_% / P_%. For example, Table 4A shows that the White parity ratio for California was 1.14, which is 44% divided by 38.5%

Comments about Table 4A – Whites in Tech

  • The numbers in the third (SS_%) and fourth (WhiTech) columns of this table, i.e., the percentage of the nation’s White techs who lived in each state and the estimated number of techs (WhiTech) in each state are equivalent to the colors on Map 4A. Indeed, the colors on this map are based on the numbers in these columns.

  • To be specific, the third and fourth columns show that in 2014 the largest number of White techs lived in California; the next largest lived in Texas. White techs were found in every state after that in smaller and smaller numbers.

  • This story is captured by the parity values in the last column (Par) of Table 4A (and in its full version). White parity values were all close to 1.0, which means that the percentage of techs who were White in each state was more or less the same as the percentage of Whites in the state’s total population. In other words, if you walked into a tech office in any state in the USA, you would probably encounter the same percentage of White techs as the percentage of White people you would encounter walking down the street – with an important exception. Random encounters with 10 White people on a street would only involve five males; but as per Table 3 (above) random encounters with 10 White techs in an office would probably involve seven or eight males.

Comments about Table 4B – Blacks in Tech

  • As I noted earlier, New York City and Washington, DC have large daytime commuter populations. Therefore the number of Black techs who worked in New York state or Washington, DC in 2014 may have been substantially larger than the number of Black techs listed in Table 4B.

  • Column 4 (BlkTech) of Table 4B (above) shows that in 2014 the largest number of Black techs lived in Texas, second largest in Georgia, third in Virginia, fourth in New York, fifth in Maryland, sixth in Florida, and California placed seventh. Why? Because in 2014 the vast majority Black Americans still lived in Eastern and Southern states; so that’s where most of the Black techs were found. This put most Black techs at a considerable disadvantage with regards to learning about and/or being recruited for tech jobs in Silicon Valley.

  • California’s prominence as the corporate headquarters of many of the nation’s richest tech companies makes it necessary to closely examine the status of its disadvantaged groups. Unfortunately the numbers were disappointing. As per the fifth column (T_%), the 2.9% Black share of tech in California was the lowest in the table by a wide margin. And its parity numbers in the last column (Par) were lower than all but one of the other nine states.

Comments about Table 4D – Hispanics in Tech

  • As per the third column (SS_%) of Table 4D, Hispanic techs were concentrated in three states: California, Texas, and Florida. The table shows that 21.03x percent lived in California, 15.53x percent lived in Texas, and 12.11x percent lived in Florida. Taken together, these three states accounted for 48.67x percent of the Hispanic techs in the entire country, i.e., about half. The other half live in the other 47 states and the District of Columbia.

  • All of the parity values of the top 10 states for Black techs shown in the last column of Table 4B are substantially higher than the parity values for all of the top 10 states for Hispanic techs shown in Table 4D … with one exception: Florida, parity = 0.69x. Florida’s parity value for Hispanic techs was more than twice the 0.26x parity value for Hispanics in California. Indeed, Table 4D shows that California had the lowest parity value of all of the top ten states for Hispanics. These considerations suggests that Hispanic techs may have encountered larger employment barriers than Black techs, especially in California.

  • Despite California’s relatively low Hispanic parity, the fifth column (T_%) of Table 4D shows that 10.1x percent of the techs who lived in California were Hispanic. This figure is remarkable because it is two to three times as high as the 2 percent to 4 percent employment rates for Hispanic techs that have been reported by Silicon Valley’s biggest firms in the last three years. It suggests that willingness to hire Hispanic techs was low throughout the state, but substantantially lower in the Valley.

  • Perhaps the Valley required higher academic qualifications than other employers. But as I pointed out in a recent note on this blog (“The California Pipelines of Silicon Valley”), IPEDS data showed that 20 percent of the combined total of White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic students enrolled in the best colleges and universities in California were Hispanic. So there is reason to be skeptical about claims that few Hispanic techs met the Valley’s higher recruiting standards.

Comments about Plots 5A, 5B, 5C, 5D, 5E, 5F

  • The total population of each state was at least 1,000 times the population of the techs in any racial/ethnic group. Therefore I ran simple regressions of techs in a group against the total population of that group divided by 1000 in all 50 states plus Washington, DC. The slopes of the regression lines provided estimates of the number of additional techs that would be found in a state if the size of the group increased by 1000.

  • As per the values of the beta1000 slopes, If each ethnic/racial group’s population increased by 1,000, Asian techs would increase by 42.84; Whites would increase by 14.28, Blacks would increase by 7.37; and Hispanics would increase by 4.13. The Asian line has the steepest slope; the Hispanic line has the flattest slope.   • This kind of simple regression implicitly assumes that the 50 states and DC were all more or less equally receptive or resistant to including more techs from the same groups. This may indeed have been true for most states. However, Table 4B shows that the Black parity for Texas = 0.8, whereas the Black parity for California = 0.51. This suggests that Florida was more receptive to Hispanic techs than California.

Given the limited scope of the data that I examined in this study, my selection criteria were rudimentary; so my conclusions should be regarded as tentative first approximations. Rather than just address this question for the disadvantaged Black, Hispanic, and female techs, I answered it for all six groups because the answers for the other three groups provided context for my conconclusions about the disadvantaged groups.

  1. Select states that had the largest number of techs in the racial/ethnic groups and the largest number Asian females and non-Asian females

Final Comments

I admit that parity was a crude measure of the opportunities in tech that states provided to the groups considered in this study. But, as per my comments about performance measures in the “Motivation” section of this report, I was not surprised that this metric assigned a wide range of values to the fify states and the District of Columbia. Nevertheless, I was surprised by how effectively this simple metric suggested which groups should be broken into subgroups in order to obtain more useful insights.

Female vs. Female
The low betas and national parity values in Table 5 that were assigned to female techs – 5.97 5.97, and 0.48 0.48 – compared to the high betas and national parity values assigned to Asian techs as a whole – 42.84 42.84 and 3.6 3.60 – suggested that I break the female category into its Asian vs. non-Asian components, as I did in Tables 4E and 4F.

  • As shown in Tables 5, the betas and national parity values for Asian females – 20.99 20.99 and 1.79 1.79 – were much higher than the values for non-Asian females – 4.34 4.34 and 0.4 0.40 In other words, Asian females received substantially greater employment opportunities than non-Asian females all over the country. So what were they doing or perceived to be doing that made them more employable in the 50 states and DC? What were their “best practices” that accounted for their higher scores?

  • This is not to say that tech treated Asian males and Asian females the same. Not at all. The betas and national parity values assigned to Asians as a whole – 42.84 42.84 and 3.6 3.60 – were much higher than the values assigned to Asian females – 20.99 20.99 and 1.79 1.79. This meant that the values assigned to Asian males had to be much higher than the values assigned to Asian females. In other words Asian males received substantially greater employment opportunities per capita than Asian females.

Hispanic vs. Hispanic

Black vs. Black
First, let me remind the reader that Table 1A showed that 12.4 12.4xxx percent of the U.S. population in 2014 was Black; and Table 1B showed that the nation’s 300,431 300,431 xxx Black tech professionals represented 7.3 7.3xxx percent of the nation’s 4,125,164 4,125,164XXX tech professionals.

  • Table 5 showed that the national parity rating for Blacks in tech was 0.59 0.59xxx, i.e., 7.3 7.3xxx divided by 12.4 12.4xxx, which was more than half, i.e., greater than 0.50. Given that the vast majority of tech jobs required bachelors degrees, this meant that, contrary to the demeaning fables perpetrated by Silicon Valley’s tech executives in their annual reports for the last three years, the nation’s Black academic pipelines were more than half-full; they were not perilously close to empty.

  • According to Table 8B, the five best states for Black tech professionals were the District of Columbia, Georgia, Virginia, Texas, and Florida. These states weren’t just Southern states; they were HBCU states, homes to the Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs) that educated the overwhelming majority of Black college graduates before the Supreme Court’s desegregation decision in 1954 and the Civil Rights legislation in 1964/65. To this day, when over 90 percent of Black students attend non-HBCUs, HBCUs still educate a large, disproportionate share of the nation’s Black graduates in the STEM majors that provide entry into careers in information technology. (See my previous note on this blog HBCUs – the Best Producers of Black Graduates in STEM)

  • Most people attend colleges that are no more than a few hundred miles from their homes, so the children of HBCU graduates have obtained STEM degrees from some of nation’s best colleges located in the HBCU states, e.g, Georgia Tech, Emory, Duke, Chapel Hill, and Austin. The presence of so many older Black techs in these states created dense, overlapping support networks of role models, mentors, and tutors for Black tech students and young Black tech graduates, networks that were reinforced by memberships in fraternities, sororities, churches, and other social groups, networks that facilitated their entry into the job market and subsequent promotion, networks that were not available to young Black techs in California and other non-HBCU states.

Asian vs. Asian
Question: Given the data presented in Map 4C and in Tables 4C, 6, and 8C, what were the best states for Asian tech??? Answer: All of them … :-)

This small joke raises a big question: How did Asian tech professionals attain such high parity values in every state?

  • Contrary to Silicon Valley’s executive myth makers, Table 4B shows that Black Americans have made good use of the expanded education and employment opportunities provided by the Supreme Court’s 1954 decision and the Civil Rights legislation that followed. But Black American academic pipelines are only half full because we are still overcoming the persistent evil legacy of hundreds of years of slavery and the decades of Jim Crow segregation that followed.

  • Fortunately for Asian Americans, the oppression they faced before the Supreme Court and Congress outlawed racial exclusions from education and the workplace was orders of magnitude less debilitating, nor was its aftermath as persistent. So facing lesser obstacles, Asian Americans have attained greater gains.

  • Indeed, the remarkable success of Asian Americans in tech’s academic pipelines and subsequent employment reinvigorates the old ideal of America as a “Land of Opportunity”. For example, as per Table 1A, Asians were only 5.3 percent of the nation’s population in 2014. But according to U.S. Department of Education, Asian undergraduate enrollments at Cal Tech and M.I.T. – arguably the best technical institutions in the nation, if not the world – are 45 percent and 25 percent, respectively. No one gets admitted to these highly competitive institutions without being very smart and being very well prepared.

  • While smarts and thorough preparation are usually sufficient to gain admission to the best colleges and universities, being hired for the best jobs can also be a function of who you know … or more precisely, as Granovetter first observed in one of the most widely cited papers ever published (“The Strength of Weak Ties”), it’s who you know who knows someone who can hire you. So the more Asian Americans that were hired, the more indirect contacts other Asian Americans obtained … assuming that all Asian techs were Asian Americans … which brings me to H-1B visas.

  • My recent telephone conversation with a member of the U.S. Census Bureau’s ACS user support team confirmed my understanding that the extensive PUMS sample data that I had downloaded did not distinguish between Americans and foreign residents. So the Asian data in Map 4C and Tables 4C, 6, and 8C combined two distinct subgroups – Asian Americans and Asian Asians who were probably in this country on H-1B work visas. These foreign tech professionals had the strongest possible “weak ties” to their future employers because they had been proactively recruited by agents of U.S. companies who persuded them to work for their U.S. clients in the U.S.

  • Table 9 (below) provides a rough indicator of the potential magnitude of this issue. The table’s first column contains the total number of tech professionals in each state. Its second column displays the total number of Asian techs in each state, as per Table 4C. Its third column contains estimates of the number of H-1B visa holders in each state, estimates that I obtained from the MyVisaJobs.com website. And its fourth column notes the H-1B workers as a percentage of Asian techs in the second column. Some of these percentages are absurd because some of the foreign workers in the third column were not from Asia, but from other parts of the world; and some were not employed as tech professionals.

  • For the final 1.0 version of this report I will download another PUMS data sample, this time with variables that indicate whether each member of the sample was a U.S. citizen and whether each member was born in Asia. Version 1.0 will include my examination of parity data that only covers Asian Americans. In a subsequent report I will examine changes in the magnitude and distribution of all foreign tech workers from all continents over the course of the last five or ten years.

Methods

I downloaded the #PEPSR6H data table from the Bureau’s American Fact Finder Website. This table could be configured to provide the Bureau’s estimates of the overall racial composition of the populations of the 50 states plus Washington DC in 2014. My step-by-step procedure for downloading the AFF data is described in Notes-Data-Raw-Maps-Plots on the GitHub repo.

American Fact Finder contained a wide range of tables, but none of them broke the information technology sector down by race, ethnicity, and state. So I also used the U.S. Census Bureau’s DataFerret Java application to download a subset of the Public Use Microdata Sample (PUMS) from the Bureau’s American Community Survey for 2014. The subset (39,692 observations) was usually large enough to make good estimates of the employee counts and percentages of tech employment for Blacks, Whites, Asians, and Hispanics in tech occupations in the 50 states and DC. (Note: For a few low population states, the ACS subset included missing values which meant that it wasn’t large enough to provide reliable estimates of the racial/ethnic breakdown of tech employment in those states.) My step-by-step procedures for selecting and downloading the ACS data and its codebook is described in Notes-Data-Download-PUMS-Sample on the GitHub repo.

Selected Data
I used the DataFerret to download a subset of the PUMS 2014 sample about the sex, state of residence, race, and Hispanic status of employees in the tech sector in all 50 states plus the District of Columbia:

  • Occupation
    When using the DataFerret app to select a sample of tech professionals from the full PUMS 2014 sample, I defined the “information technology sector” by designating the 13 standard occupational categories shown in Table 3. Eleven categories were software, two were hardware

  • Hispanic vs White (not Hispanic), Black (not Hispanic), Asian (not Hispanic)
    I configured the DataFerret to include all races in my sample. However, Hispanic is not regarded as a race, but as an ethnic/national origin, like being Irish, Egyptian, Japanese, or Nigerian. So I included the “Hispanic” variable. This variable designated each respondent’s Hispanic origin, e.g., Mexico, Chile, Puerto Rico, etc … but it also contained a “Not Hispanic” value for respondents who were not from Hispanic countries. Using this code, I distinguished between “White” vs. “White (not Hispanic)”, “Black” vs. “Black (not Hispanic)”, and “Asian” vs. “Asian (not Hispanic)” and “Others” vs “Others (non-Hispanic)”. Accordingly, my report contains data about four racial/ethnic groups: Hispanic, White (not Hispanic), Black (not Hispanic), and Asian (not Hispanic); but for brevity it merely calls them Hispanic, White, Black, and Asian.

  • Estimating population values from the sample
    Each row in the downloaded PUMS table contained the anonymized responses of a real tech employee in the ACS 2014 survey, i.e., sex, race/ethnicity, occupation, and state of residence. Each observation also contained a number called “personal weight” which is the Census Bureau’s estimate of how many people in the real population were like each person in the sample. An estimate of the total number of techs in the real population was found by adding up the personal weights of the techs in the sample. This also applied to subgroups, e.g, male vs. female, state of residencere, etc. The percentage share of a subgroup’s residence was obtained by dividing the estimated residence in the subgroup by the estimated residence in the entire group, i.e., dividing the sum of personal weights in a subgroup by the sum of personal weights in the larger group.

    Here’s the Census Bureau’s description of this process:
    “Production of Estimates” – The ACS PUMS sample is not self - weighted. To produce estimates or tabulations of characteristics from the ACS PUMS simply add the weights of all persons or housing units that possess the characteristic of interest. For instance, if the characteristic of interest is “total number of black teachers”, simply determine the race and occupation of all persons and cumulate the weights of those who match the characteristics of interest. To get estimates of proportions simply divide the weighted estimate of persons or housing units with a given characteristic by the weighted estimate of the base. For example, the proportion of “black teachers” is obtained by dividing the weighted estimate of black teachers by the PUMS estimate of teachers."
